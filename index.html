Projects

I love projects. Having been a loyal but passive reader most of my life, work on creative projects has brought the energetic side out of me while stretching my imagination and abilities.

Whether it's extracting insights out of large amounts of information or visualizing them to a broader audience, I learnt a lot working on them.

Here's a sample, in roughly chronological order:

Visualizing UCLA Stats Program (2016)

I made this interactive visualization of the UCLA Stats program to help students select classes and plan for the major or minor. Working on this project improved my design intuition and ability to visually communicate complex information.

This was the first web app that I built "from scratch", using vanilla JavaScript, D3.js, Bootstrap CSS and straightforward HTML.

I shared it with more than 700 Stats Club members and other UCLA students and got positive feedback. This also landed me my latest gig at the student newspaper as a front end developer.

Data Visualization Workshop (2016)

I ran this workshop for around 30 Stats Club members, introducing the principles of data visualization and the essential steps.

To make the workshop useful for members with different backgrounds, I provided a broad overview while also teaching specific techniques. I analyzed examples and emphasized how having a Point of View is neccessary to bring meaning out of raw data.

This workshop was also a personal experiment. For example, I decided to avoid using bullets or bolding text, relying only on spacing and font size to convey hierachical information. I also created a short Google Forms for members to provide feedback.

Take a look!

Kaggle SF Crime Contest (2015)

I participated in this machine learning contest to work with socio-geographic data and build predictive models. The biggest challenge was to estimate a probability distribution for each unseen observation, rather than just a one-shot classification.

Put simply: I had to quantify every possibility as remote as it might be. In addition, computing speed became a challenge due to size of the data.

To deal with these constraints, I tried random sampling and engineered new variables from raw data. Having done a few other Kaggle competitions, I learn the most from this.

Air Sensor Dashboard (2015)

I built this interactive dashboard of air quality data. It provides a big picture overview of CO2 level, temperature and humidity throughout the day. I also provided an easy way for users to plot the relationship between any two variables and interactively examine the raw data.

I coded this dashboard entirely in R, using vanilla R to format data and R Shiny to create the dashboard. The data came from the air quality sensors my roommate was testing for his research project.
