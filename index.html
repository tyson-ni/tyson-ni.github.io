Projects

I love side projects. Working on creative ideas born (usually) during showers has brought the spontaneous side out of me and stretched my imagination and abilities.

(For example, this webpage is a continuous project where I apply the latest web dev skills I learned to showcase my past and current experiences with potential employers, friends and family.)

Whether it's getting insights out of large amounts of information or visually presenting them to a general audience, I learnt a lot working on them.

Here's a sample:

Visualizing UCLA Stats Program (2016)

I made this visualization of the UCLA Stats program to help students select classes and plan for their major or minor. Working on this project improved my design intuition and ability to visually communicate complex information.

It was the first web app I built "from scratch", using vanilla JavaScript, D3.js, Bootstrap CSS and HTML. I shared it with more than 700 Stats Club members and received positive feedback.

Lecture Grades Blog Post (2016)

I analyzed the official grade distribution data and made interactive tools to help students compare GE classes. The article also examined interesting aspects related to GE classes with graphs and provided a way for students to lookup the statistics of any class.

Data Visualization Workshop (2016)

I ran this workshop for Stats Club members, introducing data visualization.

To make the workshop useful for members with different backgrounds, I provided a broad overview while also teaching specific techniques. We critiqued examples and discussed how having a Point of View is essential to bringing meaning out of raw data.

Kaggle SF Crime Contest (2015)

In this machine learning contest, I worked with socio-geographic data and built predictive models. The biggest challenge was to estimate a probability distribution for each observation, rather than simply provide a one-shot classification. To deal with this challenge, I tried random sampling and variable engineering. Having done a few other Kaggle competitions, I learn the most from this.

Air Sensor Dashboard (2015)

I built a dashboard to provide an overview of CO2 level, temperature and humidity. It also provided an easy way for users to examine the relationship between any two variables and interactively lookup the raw data.

UCLA DataFest (2015)

Three teammates and I participated in UCLA's annual data hackathon where we analyzed data provided by a car sales website. I picked up a lot of new tools and the event kickstarted my passion for writing code to solve data problems.

IBM Watson Case Presentation (2014)

I collaborated with three students to imagine deploying the IBM Watson AI in Chase bank branches and we suggested ways it could help transform the customer service experience.

Target Case Competition (2014)

A teammate and I developed proposals to improve Target's ability to serve the Asian American customer segment. Our presentation examined ways to target that market with better product selection and geographic distribution.

Honduras Microfinance Trip (2014)

I was part of a group of ten students who traveled to a Honduran village to learn from and advise a local microfinance co-op. We interviewed community members and developed proposals to sustain the co-op's cash flow and improve member participation rate.

Bathrooms of UCLA (2015)

Inspired by a blog post on a fun type of visualization called Chernoff Faces, I rated bathrooms at UCLA and made these faces to represent variables like cleanliness, privacy, etc.
