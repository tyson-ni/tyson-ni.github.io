Projects

I love side projects. Working on creative ideas born (usually) during showers has brought the spontaneous side out of me and stretched my imagination and abilities.

(For example, this webpage is a continuous project where I apply the latest web dev skills I learned to showcase my past and current experiences with potential employers, friends and family.)

Whether it's getting insights out of large amounts of information or visually presenting them to a general audience, I learnt a lot working on them.

Here's a sample:

Visualizing UCLA Stats Program (2016)

I made this visualization of the UCLA Stats program to help students select classes and plan for their major or minor. Working on this project improved my design intuition and ability to visually communicate complex information.

It was the first web app I built "from scratch", using vanilla JavaScript, D3.js, Bootstrap CSS and HTML.

I shared it with more than 700 Stats Club members and got positive feedback. It also landed me my latest gig at Daily Bruin as a front end developer.

Lecture Grades Blog Post (2016)

I analyzed the official grade distribution data and made an interactive chart to help students compare GE classes.

The article also examined interesting aspects related to GE classes with graphs and provided a way for students to lookup the statistics of any class. It was my first collaboration with other developers at Daily Bruin.

Data Visualization Workshop (2016)

I ran this workshop for Stats Club members, introducing data visualization.

To make the workshop useful for members with different backgrounds, I provided a broad overview while also teaching specific techniques. We critiqued examples and discussed how having a Point of View is essential to bringing meaning out of raw data.

Take a look!

Kaggle SF Crime Contest (2015)

I participated in this machine learning contest, worked with socio-geographic data and built predictive models. The biggest challenge was to estimate a probability distribution for each observation, rather than simply provide a one-shot classification.

Put simply: I had to quantify every possibility as remote as it might be. In addition, computing speed became a challenge due to size of the data.

To deal with these constraints, I tried random sampling and engineered new geographic variables. Having done a few other Kaggle competitions, I learn the most from this.

Air Sensor Dashboard (2015)

I built a dashboard of air quality data. It provided an overview of CO2 level, temperature and humidity throughout a 3-day period. I also designed an easy way for users to plot the relationship between any two variables and interactively examine the raw data.

I coded this dashboard using the R Shiny framework. The data came from air quality sensors my roommate was testing for his research project.

UCLA DataFest (2015)

Three teammates and I participated in UCLA's annual data hackathon where we analyzed data provided by a car sales website. This was my first experience analyzing a huge dataset in a short period of time. As a beginner in R, I learnt a lot of new R techniques and started to like it. We made a few graphics and competed in the Best Visualization category.

IBM Watson Case Presentation (2014)

I collaborated with three students to imagine deploying the IBM Watson AI in Chase bank branches and we suggested ways it could help transform the customer service experience.

Target Case Competition (2014)

A teammate and I developed proposals to improve Target's ability to serve the Asian American customer segment. Our presentation examined ways to target the market with better product selection and closer geographic proximity.

Honduras Microfinance Trip (2014)

I was part of a group of ten UC students who traveled to a Honduran village to learn from and advise a local microfinance co-op. We interviewed community members and developed proposals to sustain the co-op's cash flow and improve member participation rate.

We were there to listen. For example, we realized after a few interviews that our job wasn't to ask the "best" question or to suggest quick fixes. Rather, it was essential to ask open-ended but focused questions that would make villagers share their stories.

Bathrooms of UCLA (2015)

Inspired by a blog post on a fun type of visualization called Chernoff Faces, I rated a few male bathrooms at difference places in UCLA and generated these faces to represent multiple variables like cleanliness, privacy, etc.
