Projects

I love projects. Having been a loyal but passive reader most of my life, work on creative projects has brought the energetic side out of me while stretching my imagination and abilities.

Whether it's extracting insights out of large amounts of information or visually presenting them to a general audience, I learnt a lot working on projects.

Here's a sample, in (roughly) chronological order:

Visualizing UCLA Stats Program (2016)

I made this visualization of the UCLA Stats program to help students select classes and plan for the major or minor. Working on this project improved my design intuition and ability to visually communicate complex information.

This was the first web app I built "from scratch", using vanilla JavaScript, D3.js, Bootstrap CSS and HTML.

I shared it with more than 700 Stats Club members and got positive feedback. It also landed me my latest gig at Daily Bruin as a front end developer.

Lecture Grades Blog Post (2016)

I analyzed the official grade distribution data and made an interactive chart to help students compare GE classes.

In addition, I wrote about interesting aspects related to GE classes using graphs and provided a way for students to lookup the statistics of any class. This article was my first collaboration with other developers at Daily Bruin.

Data Visualization Workshop (2016)

I ran this workshop for Stats Club members, introducing data visualization.

To make the workshop useful for members with different backgrounds, I provided a broad overview while also teaching specific techniques. We critiqued examples and discussed how having a Point of View is essential to bringing meaning out of raw data.

Take a look!

Kaggle SF Crime Contest (2015)

I participated in this machine learning contest to work with socio-geographic data and build predictive models. The biggest challenge was to estimate a probability distribution for each observation, rather than simply provide a one-shot classification.

Put simply: I had to quantify every possibility as remote as it might be. In addition, computing speed became a challenge due to size of the data.

To deal with these constraints, I tried random sampling and engineered new geographic variables. Having done a few other Kaggle competitions, I learn the most from this.

Air Sensor Dashboard (2015)

I built this dashboard of air quality data. It provided an overview of CO2 level, temperature and humidity throughout a 3-day period. I also designed an easy way for users to plot the relationship between any two variables and interactively examine the raw data.

I coded this dashboard entirely in R, using vanilla R to format data and R Shiny to create the dashboard. The data came from air quality sensors my roommate was testing for his research project.

Target Case Competition (2014)

A teammate and I developed proposals to improve Target's ability to serve the Asian American customer segment. Our presentation examined ways to target the market with better product selection and closer geographic proximity.

Honduras Microfinance Trip (2014)

I was part of a group of ten UC students who traveled to a Honduras village to learn from and advise a local microfinance co-op. We interviewed community members and developed investment proposals to sustain the co-op's cash flow and improve member participation rate.

We were there to listen. For example, we realized during after a few interviews that our job wasn't to ask the "sharpest" question or to suggest quick fixes. Rather, I learnt that it was important to ask open-ended but focused questions that would make villagers tell their stories.
